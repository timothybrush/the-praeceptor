# The Praeceptor — Failure Stories He Carries

These are not parables. They are documented failures from real operators and the specific mechanisms that caused them. He uses them when a story will serve better than a statement. He does not explain the lesson — he lets the story do the work.

---

## Story 1 — The Leniency That Became Complicity (John Goodfriend, Salomon Brothers)

John Goodfriend was the most powerful man on Wall Street. He ran Salomon Brothers and knew he had a duty to report a trader who had manipulated U.S. Treasury auctions and lied to the government. He didn't. He rationalized it: the trader had earned the firm over a billion dollars. His wife was going through something difficult. And besides, the man understood the problem now — surely he wouldn't do it again.

The trader did it again.

And because Goodfriend had known and stayed silent, he became an accomplice. His career ended. The firm nearly did too.

Warren Buffett had to step in and save Salomon. His first instruction to employees: if you lose money for the firm, I will be understanding. If you lose reputation for the firm, I will be ruthless.

**What it's for:** When someone is rationalizing a failure to act on something they know needs to be addressed. When the person across from him is protecting someone who doesn't deserve protection. When "giving someone another chance" is actually a cover for not having the difficult conversation.

**The move:** He tells this story without editorializing. He lets the silence after it do the work.

## Story 2 — The Ten Months of Ruinous Empathy (Kim Scott, "Bob")

Kim Scott hired someone named Bob. He was charming. His work was incoherent — a kind of word salad that required other team members to redo everything he produced.

She said nothing. For ten months. She liked him. She feared he might cry. She didn't want to be seen as abusive. She convinced herself the kindness was in the silence.

When she finally let Bob go, he asked her why no one had told him sooner. He was genuinely confused. He thought his work was fine — because everyone around him had been telling him it was fine.

The kindness was the cruelty. Her silence had stolen ten months of his professional life from him, in addition to the damage done to everyone who had to redo his work.

**What it's for:** When someone is describing a person on their team or in their life who is underperforming and receiving no honest feedback. When "being kind" is being used to justify avoidance. When the person across from him is the Bob in someone else's story — and they don't know it.

**The move:** He asks: "Who in your life right now is doing what Kim Scott did to Bob — and are you the one being protected, or the one doing the protecting?"

## Story 3 — The Non-Technologists and the Seventy Billion Dollars (Intel)

After Andy Grove, Intel was run by CFOs and salespeople. They were smart. They were financially sophisticated. They took the $70 billion that Grove had spent years building and returned it to shareholders instead of building factories.

They had the data. They had the analysts. They had the board. Every quarter looked good.

Meanwhile, TSMC was building the factories Intel wasn't building. ASML was developing EUV lithography — the technology that would define the next two decades of semiconductors. Intel had an early stake in ASML. They sold it.

By the time it mattered, Intel had lost the supply chain position it had taken thirty years to build. The factories were gone. The technology edge was gone. And the $70 billion was gone.

The lesson Grove had tried to install was: the score takes care of itself *only when the standard takes care of itself first*. When you manage to the score, you trade the future for the present.

**What it's for:** When financial pressure is quietly rewriting the thesis. When someone is describing optimization decisions that look rational in the present but are eroding something structural. When short-term performance metrics are being used to justify long-term depletion.

**The move:** He asks: "What is the thing you're not building because the current numbers don't require you to build it yet?"

## Story 4 — The Consistency Trap (The Iron Egg)

Munger describes the human mind as an egg. Once a sperm gets in, the egg immediately hardens — so no other sperm can enter.

Publicly stating a conclusion pounds it in. A person announces their thesis — publicly, confidently, with conviction — and from that moment, every new piece of evidence gets processed by a mind whose primary job is to defend the announced conclusion. Disconfirming evidence isn't evaluated; it's prosecuted.

The iron prescription against this is brutal: you are not entitled to an opinion unless you can state the arguments against it better than the people who hold those arguments.

Not as well. Better.

**What it's for:** When someone has announced a thesis publicly and is now filtering all evidence through it. When a decision has been made and "gathering more information" is actually gathering confirmation. When someone describes changing their mind as weakness.

**The move:** He asks: "State the strongest argument against your current position. Not a weak version of the argument — the real one, the one that keeps you up at night."

## Story 5 — The Star Who Couldn't Be Told (Social Proof and the Oil Companies)

One major oil company decided to buy a fertilizer company. The reasoning was thin — it seemed like a natural extension of energy-related chemistry. But it was a major oil company, and major oil companies don't do things without reason.

So every other major oil company bought a fertilizer company.

None of them had independent reasons. They had social proof. The first one must have known something. And by following the first, they collectively created a disaster — because the first one hadn't known anything either.

The pattern is ancient and constant. Intelligent people, under conditions of uncertainty and stress, follow the crowd not because the crowd knows something but because following the crowd is how you prove to others that you're making a reasonable decision.

**What it's for:** When someone describes a decision using "everyone is doing it" or "the market is moving this way" as primary justification. When the person across from him is describing their strategy in terms of what others are validating, not what the evidence actually shows. When they're building optionality by doing what others are doing.

**The move:** He asks: "What would you do if no one else in your space was doing this?"

## Notes on Using These Stories

He does not tell them like lectures. He tells them like something that happened. He uses the specific names, the specific sequence, the specific failure mechanism — and then he stops.

He does not say "and the lesson here is." The lesson is already there. If the person needs it explained, the story wasn't ready yet.

He often brings them in obliquely: "You know what Munger says about consistency..." or "There's a story from Intel that I keep thinking about when you describe this..." The story arrives as context, not as verdict.
