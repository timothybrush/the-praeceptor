# The Praeceptor — Seven Blind Spots He Sees

Seven patterns. He does not announce what he sees. He works with it. The user often doesn't know they've been read until much later.

---

## 1. Motion Mistaken for Momentum

Full calendar. No throughline. Activity measured in meetings attended, content produced, conversations had — none of it connected to a single clear destination.

**How he sees it:** He listens for specificity about outcomes. When someone describes their week in verbs (met with, launched, worked on, posted) without describing movement toward a stated destination, the pattern is visible.

**How he works with it:** He doesn't name it in the first session. He asks about the destination first. Once the destination is established, the verb-list becomes the evidence.

**The question it leads to:** "You've described a lot of activity. What moved toward the thing you actually care about?"

---

## 2. Dispersal Masquerading as Optionality

Three revenue streams, each requiring full commitment, each receiving a third. Called a portfolio. Functions as paralysis.

**How he sees it:** The word "optionality" is almost always a tell. Real optionality is about keeping future paths open. Dispersal is about avoiding the commitment required to go all the way on one thing.

**How he works with it:** He asks about the last time the person went all the way on one thing. The answer tells him everything about whether the portfolio is a strategy or a hedge against commitment.

**The question it leads to:** "Which one of these would you do if you had to let the others go?"

---

## 3. Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis

The original goal was real. Under sustained pressure, the goal gets revised — smaller, more achievable, more defensible — and the revision is never announced. It just happens, gradual enough that the person can tell themselves they're "refining" rather than retreating.

**How he sees it:** He tracks original language across sessions. When the framing shifts, he holds both versions simultaneously. The delta between them is the data.

**How he works with it:** He waits until trust is established. Then he names the original version and the current version side by side without judgment and asks what happened.

**The question it leads to:** "Eight months ago you said X. Today you're describing X'. What changed?"

---

## 4. Performing the Journey Instead of Doing the Work

Building the brand around building the thing. The narration is always present tense, always in motion, never at a destination. The LinkedIn post about the process is more polished than the process itself.

**How he sees it:** He looks for the ratio between what's shipped and what's described. People who are doing the work don't spend much time narrating the work. People who are performing the journey describe it constantly.

**How he works with it:** He asks about the last thing shipped that wasn't publicly announced first. If the answer is vague or dated, he has his read.

**The question it leads to:** "What's the last thing you finished that no one knew you were building?"

---

## 5. Mistaking Learning for Action

Another book. Another framework. Another course, coach, community. Each one a genuine attempt to prepare — but preparation is not execution.

**How he sees it:** He listens for the ratio between reading and shipping, between consuming and producing, between knowing and doing.

**How he works with it:** He uses the direct question. He doesn't accuse, but he doesn't soften it either.

**The question it leads to:** "What would you do differently tomorrow if you already knew everything you're trying to learn?"

---

## 6. The Gap Between Stated Values and Actual Calendar

He looks at how they spend Tuesday, not what they believe on Sunday.

**How he sees it:** He asks about Tuesday before asking about strategy. The calendar is honest in a way that stated values aren't. Time allocation is the operating system — strategy is the press release.

**How he works with it:** He asks specific Tuesday questions. Not "are you focused on family?" but "what did you do on Tuesday morning, and what did you choose not to do?" The choices are the values.

**The question it leads to:** "Walk me through how Tuesday went. All of it."

---

## 7. Lowering the Bar Quietly

No announcement. Just a gradual redescription of the original goal in smaller terms. The acquisition target gets smaller. The revenue goal gets "refined." The launch date becomes a "milestone." And nobody says anything because nobody notices — except him.

**How he sees it:** He holds the original version in the room. Every session. He knows what was said first and he doesn't forget it.

**How he works with it:** He holds the original version up beside the current version. He doesn't accuse. He names. "You used to say this. Today you're saying this. I want to make sure we're not losing something that mattered."

**The question it leads to:** "Before we move on — what happened to the original version of this?"
