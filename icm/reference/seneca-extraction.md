# Seneca — Mentor Mechanics Extraction
## Source: Letters from a Stoic (Epistulae Morales)

---

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. What's the smallest version you can do today?**
Seneca's urgency principle applied: "Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est." Everything is alien; only time is ours. The person who says "I'll do this when [condition]" is trading a known present for a hypothetical future that may never arrive. The move: "What's the smallest version of that thing you could do today? Not eventually — today. If you can't name it, the plan is a story about a future self, not a plan."

**2. Name the time leak.**
Seneca opens his first letter: most of our time is taken — some by force, some by negligence, some by others, and the rest slips away while we're not looking. People do not feel time being taken because it happens slowly, in small increments. The move: "Walk me through last week. Where did the time actually go?" Not the planned calendar — the actual hours. The gap between the two is the leak. The leak is always bigger than people think.

**3. Live as if time could end today — and postpone nothing important.**
Seneca's prescription: "Ita fac, mi Lucili: vindica te tibi." Claim yourself back. Don't live a life entirely given to others' demands, always pending some future moment when you'll finally begin. The move: "What is the thing you've been postponing until the circumstances are better? What would you do differently this week if you knew next week wouldn't come?" The urgency isn't to panic. It's to act now on what matters.

**4. Examine the quality of the life, not just its length.**
Seneca distinguished between living long and living well. A life of distraction is a long life not fully inhabited. The question is not "how many years?" but "how many of those years were fully lived?" The move: "How much of your time this month was spent on the things that actually matter to you — not what you say matters, but what you'd point to as meaningful? What was the ratio?"

**5. The pre-meditation of difficulty.**
Seneca's practice: imagine the difficult thing that might happen, and inhabit it in advance. Not to catastrophize — to remove its power to surprise. Most fear comes from unexpectedness. The person who has already imagined the failure, the loss, the setback — and has lived through it mentally — is not controlled by the fear of it. The move: "What is the worst version of this that could actually happen? Inhabit it now. Live through it in this conversation. How does it change what you do today?"

## Diagnostic Questions

- "What's the smallest version of this you could do today — not eventually, today?" — The urgency move.
- "Walk me through where your time actually went last week. Not the plan — the reality." — The time leak.
- "What would you do differently this week if you knew next week wouldn't come?" — Mortality urgency.
- "What's the worst version of this that could actually happen? Inhabit it now." — Pre-meditation.
- "How much of this month did you spend on what actually matters to you?" — Quality vs. quantity.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The man who always intended to live.**
Seneca writes: "Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est." The person who spends their life being busy — responding to others' demands, accumulating things, maintaining obligations — and who never quite gets to the things that were supposed to matter. Not malicious. Not lazy. Just constantly displaced. The insight: "They lost nothing because they never had it. They never owned themselves." This is not a story about death. It's a story about a life that happened to someone while they were busy making other plans.

**The indefinitely postponed beginning.**
Seneca: "Recede in te ipse quantum potes." Retreat into yourself as much as you can. The person who has been preparing to begin — accumulating resources, conditions, knowledge, the right moment — for years. The moment never arrives because it never was the point. The preparation became the avoidance. The move: name the date by which you'll have done this. If you can't name a date, it isn't a plan.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → The time leak diagnostic is Seneca's most direct tool against motion without momentum. The hours say what matters; the stated priority doesn't.
- **Mistaking Learning for Action** → "What would you do differently if you already knew everything you're trying to learn?" is a Senecan question. The preparation is endless because beginning is being avoided.
- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → The original goal gets postponed when pressure mounts. Seneca: if you keep postponing, you'll find you've postponed your whole life. Name the smallest version you can do today.
- **The Gap Between Stated Values and Actual Calendar** → "Only time is ours." The calendar is the honest biography. Not the stated values — the actual hours.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → The quiet revision happens in time: the thing that was urgent becomes "eventually." Name it. "When did you stop treating this as urgent? What changed?"
