# Daniel Kahneman — Mentor Mechanics Extraction
## Source: Thinking, Fast and Slow

---

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Name the system doing the thinking.**
Before engaging with any plan, decision, or diagnosis: identify which system is driving it. System 1 is fast, intuitive, associative — it produces a feeling of certainty without the work of certainty. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful. Most people believe they're running System 2 when they're running System 1. The move: "How long did it take you to arrive at that conclusion? How many alternatives did you genuinely consider?" Fast answers to hard questions are almost always System 1 dressed as System 2.

**2. Ask for the base rate before accepting the story.**
When someone presents a plan as unique or exceptional: "What is the reference class for this? Of companies / projects / people in exactly this situation, what percentage succeeded?" The specific story they're telling themselves is compelling. The statistical category it belongs to is not. Kahneman calls this "the inside view" vs. "the outside view." The inside view is always more vivid. The outside view is almost always more accurate.

**3. Invert the question — do a pre-mortem.**
Before commitment to a major decision: "Imagine it's 12 months from now and this completely failed. What happened?" The pre-mortem removes the bias toward optimism by making the failure real first, then backward-engineering causes. People who have committed to a plan cannot easily imagine its failure (consistency and commitment tendency). Imagining it as already failed bypasses that resistance. Collect the explanations. Now look at the plan.

**4. Separate luck from skill in any outcome analysis.**
When someone interprets a recent success or failure as evidence of their approach: "How much of this result regresses to the mean?" Exceptional performance — in either direction — tends to revert. The coach who praised a great performance and then saw it get worse was not experiencing cause and effect. They were experiencing regression. The move: "If you ran this exact situation 10 times, what would the distribution of outcomes look like?"

**5. Identify the substitution.**
When someone is answering a hard question confidently: identify the easier question they're actually answering. "How confident are you?" is often answered by "How fluent does this feel?" "Is this a good investment?" is often answered by "Do I like this company?" The substitution produces an answer — and the answer feels correct — but it was generated by a different question than the one that was asked. The move: "What question did you just answer?"

## Diagnostic Questions

- "How did you arrive at that conclusion? How long did it take?" — Probes System 1 vs. System 2.
- "What's the reference class? What percentage of similar situations end like you expect?" — Pulls in the outside view.
- "Imagine this fails. What happened?" — Pre-mortem. Forces genuine consideration of failure before commitment.
- "What question did you just answer?" — Substitution check.
- "If this same exact situation played out 10 times, what would the range of outcomes look like?" — Regression to mean.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The tank crews and the overconfident instructors.**
Israeli military flight instructors praised cadets for excellent performances and scolded them for poor ones. Then they observed: cadets seemed to get worse after praise and better after criticism. Conclusion: harsh criticism works better than praise. Wrong. This is regression to the mean. After exceptional performance (in either direction), performance regresses. The instructors were attributing to their own behavior what was caused by statistical reversion. They drew a causal story from random variation.

**WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is.**
A compelling narrative built from limited data feels complete because you are not aware of what you're missing. The confidence people feel in a judgment is determined by the coherence of the story — not by the quality or quantity of the information. People are not troubled by gaps in evidence. They simply don't notice them. The move: "What information would change your conclusion? What are you missing that you're not aware of missing?"

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → System 1 produces the feeling of progress. System 2 is needed to check whether the direction is correct.
- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → The inside view is seductive because it's vivid. The outside view (base rates, reference class) is what holds the original standard.
- **Mistaking Learning for Action** → Pre-mortems are learning that's directly coupled to action — they change the decision being made, not just the knowledge held.
- **Performing the Journey** → WYSIATI: the story of building feels as real as the building itself to System 1. Asking "what has shipped?" bypasses this.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → Substitution: the hard question ("are we at the original goal?") gets replaced by the easy question ("do things feel better than before?"). Name the swap.
