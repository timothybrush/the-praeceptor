## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Name the pyramid.**
When a team is underperforming, the problem is almost never what they say it is. The real problem lives in the pyramid: absence of trust at the base → fear of conflict → lack of commitment → avoidance of accountability → inattention to results. Before diagnosing any team problem, identify which layer is compromised. Ask: "What's the real conversation that nobody is having?" That question points to the layer that's broken.

**2. Force artificial conflict before forced agreement.**
False consensus is worse than active disagreement. When a group reaches agreement too quickly — especially on something important — slow it down. "Who has a serious objection? I want to hear the strongest argument against this." Silence in a meeting is not agreement. It's usually: protected jobs, feared reactions, accumulated resentment. The move: make it safe for the dissent to exist before the decision is made, not after.

**3. Name the unspoken individual commitment.**
After any decision: "What is each person in this room committing to? Specifically. By when?" Not "we agreed to move forward" — but "you, specifically, are doing X, and by this date." Public and specific commitments activate accountability that general agreement never does. The failure mode: decisions that everyone attended and nobody owns.

**4. Disagree and commit — explicitly.**
When someone is reluctant to commit after losing the argument: "You can disagree with the decision and still commit to executing it. These are not the same thing. Which one is happening right now?" Great teams can hold both states simultaneously — genuine disagreement on record, full commitment to the direction. The thing that destroys teams is the third state: silent non-commitment that masquerades as agreement. Name it before leaving the room.

**5. Hold the mirror on accountability avoidance.**
When individuals are avoiding calling out peers: "Why won't you say directly to that person what you're saying to me about them?" Accountability within a team is horizontal, not vertical. The team member who says nothing about a colleague's missed commitment is not being kind — they are protecting themselves from an uncomfortable conversation at the team's expense. The move: "Is this about their feelings, or yours?"

## Diagnostic Questions

- "What's the conversation nobody is having in this room right now?" — The core Lencioni diagnostic.
- "What did you personally commit to? Specifically?" — Accountability check.
- "Who has a serious objection? I want to hear the real argument against this." — Conflict activation.
- "Are you disagreeing, or are you not committing? These are different." — Commitment vs. dissent separation.
- "Why are you telling me this instead of telling them?" — Accountability avoidance mirror.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The DecisionTech executive team (from the fable).**
Kathryn inherits a high-performing team on paper — talented executives, good compensation, market position — that is silently destroying its chances. Their dysfunction is invisible to them: they avoid conflict because they fear damaging relationships; they give lip service to decisions they privately undermine; they protect their individual departments instead of the company. They are smart, experienced, and completely ineffective as a unit. The insight: a team of talented individuals performing for their own metrics will always underperform a team of good-enough people performing for the collective result.

**The leadership team that agreed — and didn't.**
A decision is made in a meeting. Everyone nods. No objections. Two weeks later, some people are actively working against the decision in their own departments — not because they changed their minds, but because they never actually committed. They attended and nodded. Attending is not committing. The Lencioni move: end every meeting with explicit, named, individual commitments. If someone can't commit, surface it now — not in their behavior two weeks later.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → Teams in false consensus produce enormous activity with no shared direction. The pyramid diagnosis reveals which layer is decoupling effort from result.
- **Dispersal vs. Optionality** → The team protecting individual department metrics is the organizational version of dispersal. Each person is maximizing their own score; no one is maximizing the team's.
- **Performing the Journey vs. Doing the Work** → Meetings that produce records of agreement without named individual commitments are performing alignment. Ask what each person is doing differently on Monday morning.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → The team that stops holding each other accountable has lowered the bar — each missed commitment a small increment down. Name it before it normalizes.
