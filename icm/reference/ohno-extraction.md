## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Go to where the work actually happens — not where the reports say it happens.**
Ohno's first principle: stand in the circle. He would draw a chalk circle on the factory floor and tell managers to stand in it for hours, observing. Not reading reports. Not in meetings. Watching what actually happens. The move: "When did you last stand where the work is actually done — not the summary of it? What did the reports say versus what the floor said?" The gap between those two things is always where the problem lives.

**2. Ask why five times — not once.**
The Five Whys is not a method for finding excuses. It is a method for finding the actual cause, which is almost never the visible cause. The visible cause is a symptom. The actual cause is the condition that keeps generating the symptom. The move: "Tell me the problem. Now: why did that happen? And why did that happen? And why did that happen?" Don't stop at the first plausible answer. The first plausible answer is usually the surface. Go deeper until the answer points to something that, if changed, means the problem can't recur.

**3. Pull, don't push.**
Ohno's system insight: most organizations push — they produce what they planned to produce, then try to sell it. This generates waste: inventory, overproduction, waiting, defects discovered late. The discipline is to pull — produce only what has actually been requested, when it's actually been requested, in the quantity actually needed. The move: "Are you building this because someone pulled it — because there's actual demand right now — or because you planned to build it? If it's the second: who decided there would be demand, and when?"

**4. Stop the line — stop everything — when something is wrong.**
Jidoka: the machine stops the moment it detects a defect. Not at the end of the run. Not at the next quality check. Now. The insight: passing a defect downstream multiplies the cost. Stopping is not inefficiency — continuing is. The move: "If you already see something wrong with this — not a concern, a real signal — what would it take to stop? What's keeping you from stopping right now?"

**5. Eliminate waste before adding capacity.**
Ohno's seven wastes: overproduction, waiting, transport, overprocessing, inventory, motion, defects. Most organizations add people, add systems, add investment when things aren't working. Ohno's question came first: what's already being wasted? The move: "Before adding anything — before hiring, before buying, before building — where is waste already in the system? Name each one. The waste is the capacity you already have and aren't using."

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The manager who fixed the machine but not the system.**
Ohno tells the story of a machine that kept breaking down. The fix was always the same: repair it, restart, continue. The machine broke down again. The repair was correct but not complete — it addressed the symptom without examining why the machine kept reaching the same failure state. Five Whys applied: the actual cause was a maintenance interval that had never been examined because the machine "usually worked." The problem was never the machine. It was the assumption that "usually working" was good enough.

**The factory that produced what it planned instead of what was needed.**
Before the Toyota Production System, the prevailing model was batch-and-queue: build large quantities, store them, sell from inventory. This looked efficient because machines ran continuously. Ohno saw it as pure waste — the machines were producing things that sat in warehouses while actual customer orders waited. The real cost of production appeared only at the end, when inventory sat unsold or when defects discovered in month three turned out to have been produced in month one. The waste was invisible to the people inside the system because they were measuring the wrong thing.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → Ohno's waste taxonomy is the most precise map of motion-without-momentum ever written. "Transportation," "waiting," "overprocessing" are motion. Pull, jidoka, and value-stream flow are momentum.
- **Mistaking Learning for Action** → The Five Whys is the antidote to premature explanation. "I know why this happened" after one why is almost always wrong. Go five deep before you think you understand.
- **Performing the Journey** → Go to gemba. The person who reports on the factory without going to the factory is performing operations management. The work is in the circle, watching.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → Jidoka requires stopping the line — refusing to pass a defect downstream. Lowering the bar quietly is the opposite: passing it downstream and calling it acceptable.
- **Dispersal vs. Optionality** → The pull question: three production lines at one-third capacity each is overproduction three times over if nobody's pulling from them. The discipline is to go deep where demand actually exists.
