# Robert Greene — Mentor Mechanics Extraction
## Source: Laws of Human Nature (interview transcript + book)

---

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Look for the pattern, not the incident.**
Greene's core diagnostic principle: "Nobody ever does anything once." When someone describes a difficult person, a failed relationship, a business that ended badly — ask about the pattern, not the event. "Has this happened before with other people in a similar role?" If yes, the pattern is information. The person is either attracting something, creating something, or missing a signal they've seen before. The move: "This situation you're describing — where else in your life has a version of this happened?"

**2. Read what isn't being said.**
Most important human communication happens below the surface of words. Micro-expressions, what's conspicuously absent, what's deflected rather than addressed — these are the actual content. From the transcript: "There's no one more dangerous than a person who has no self-awareness." The move: what did they NOT address when talking about this situation? What did they circle around and not name? The absence is the data.

**3. Turn self-focus outward.**
Greene's consistent diagnosis of failure: "The source of most pain in people's life, why they hit a wall, is because they don't understand people." People who are stuck are stuck inside their own perspective. They can describe their own experience precisely and the other person's experience not at all. The move: "Tell me how this situation looks from their side. Not what they did to you — what were they experiencing, what did they want, what were they afraid of?" The answer reveals whether the problem is understanding or character.

**4. Name the shadow — specifically, without accusation.**
Every person carries impulses they haven't acknowledged: envy, narcissism, desire for control, fear of being ordinary. Greene: "We are all on the spectrum of self-absorption." The toxic person denies it categorically. The strong-character person names it: "I sent this to 40 people and asked 'which one do you think is me?'" The move: when you see a shadow pattern operating in someone's behavior, name it specifically, then ask: "Do you recognize that in yourself, even slightly?" The recognition is the whole game. Everything follows from it.

**5. Read character through pattern of past behavior, not through stated intention.**
"People leave traces of who they are. You're just not picking up the signs." The person who has done something harmful once has almost certainly done it before — and they will do it again. The stated intention ("I've never done this before, I won't do it again") is not evidence; it is the exact thing every person in that position says. The move when someone is considering a major trust decision: "What do their past actions tell you, independent of what they're saying now?"

## Diagnostic Questions

- "Where else in your life has a version of this happened?" — Pattern recognition.
- "Tell me what this looks like from their perspective." — Turning self-focus outward.
- "What is the impulse in yourself that you haven't named yet?" — Shadow work, without accusation.
- "What did the pattern of their past behavior tell you before this happened?" — Character reading.
- "What are they NOT saying when they tell you this story?" — Reading absence.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The CEO with no self-control.**
Greene served on the board of a company run by a brilliant entrepreneur who could not censor himself — described his sex life to reporters, yelled at people, made enemies systematically. He was a great designer. He could not build a sustainable company. "He thought that anything he touches is going to be brilliant and great. He doesn't know his own limits." Greene helped remove him. The company collapsed. The failure: a person who mistakes talent in one domain for immunity from limitation in all others. The move: "Where are your limits? Who on your team covers for the places where you're over your head?"

**The envy that masquerades as criticism.**
When someone criticizes your success in a way that "gets under your skin" — the criticism isn't really about you. It's about what your success makes them feel about themselves. "They devalue it because it threatens something in them." The tell: effusive praise that doesn't feel earned, or criticism that focuses on your motives rather than your work. The Greene move: recognize it as envy, don't take the bait, redirect attention back to the work.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Dispersal vs. Optionality** → People who can't commit are often running from a fear they haven't named. The dispersal is the symptom. The unnamed fear is the cause. Ask what they're afraid will happen if they go all the way on one thing.
- **Performing the Journey** → The person who narrates building but doesn't build is running from the terror of actually being evaluated. The performance is protective. Name it without accusation: "What does it feel like when you imagine shipping this and getting feedback?"
- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → The pattern: what does this person do every time pressure mounts? That's not a recent behavior — it's a character pattern. Where have they retreated before?
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → The toxic person always says "I've never done this before." They always have. The pattern reading move: track their behavior under pressure over time, not their stated intentions under observation.
