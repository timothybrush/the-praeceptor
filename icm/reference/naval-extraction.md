# Naval Ravikant — Mentor Mechanics Extraction
## Source: The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (book) + Naval Podcast Transcript

---

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. The one-sentence test.**
Naval's clarity diagnostic: "If you can't say it in one sentence, you haven't decided yet." Long explanations of what you're doing, why you're doing it, and what it means are not clarity — they are deferred clarity. Real decisions compress. The person who needs three paragraphs to explain their strategy hasn't arrived at it yet. The move: "Say what you're doing in one sentence. If you can't, what are you avoiding by not being able to say it simply?"

**2. Diagnose: renting time or building equity?**
Naval's core wealth distinction: there are two ways to generate income — trade time for money (employment, freelancing), or build something whose output scales beyond your direct effort (equity in a business, a product, intellectual property). Most people mix the two without realizing it, which means they get neither the stability of the first nor the leverage of the second. The move: "Which of these is this? Are you renting your time, or building something that could produce value when you're not working on it?"

**3. The specific knowledge test.**
Specific knowledge is what you know that can't be easily taught, can't be automated, and feels like play to you while it feels like work to others. It's the intersection of your genuine curiosity, your accumulated skills, and the world's needs. When someone is choosing a path: "What do you do that doesn't feel like work to you, but that others would pay for? That's the specific knowledge. Everything else is rented time with a ceiling." The move: identify the specific knowledge before building any strategy around it.

**4. Separate ownership, accountability, and leverage — where are you on each?**
Naval's wealth framework: you need all three. Ownership (equity, not just employment). Accountability (your name on the outcome, not just the execution). Leverage (labor, capital, code, or media — forces that multiply your effort without proportional effort increase). Most people have accountability but neither ownership nor leverage. The move: "Of these three — ownership, accountability, leverage — which ones do you have? Which ones are you missing? What would it take to get the missing ones?"

**5. Health, then relationships, then work — in that order.**
Naval's priority stack: most people optimize for work first, relationships second, and health last. This is backwards. Health is the foundation. Without it, nothing else compounds. Relationships, because the quality of the people around you determines your ceiling. Work last — because bad work can be undone; bad health and broken relationships are much harder to recover from. The move: "Which of these three is your calendar actually optimized for? Not what you say — what Tuesday looked like."

## Diagnostic Questions

- "Say what you're doing in one sentence. If you can't — what are you avoiding?" — Clarity test.
- "Are you renting your time or building equity? Which is this?" — Time vs. leverage.
- "What's your specific knowledge — the thing that feels like play to you but work to everyone else?" — Specific knowledge.
- "Of ownership, accountability, leverage — which do you have? Which are you missing?" — Wealth framework.
- "What does your health look like? Not your plan — your last two weeks." — Priority stack.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The employee who built someone else's dream.**
Naval: most people spend their careers building leverage for other people. They're accountable — their name is on the work — but they have no ownership and no leverage of their own. When the company is sold, or the industry shifts, or the position disappears, they have nothing that belongs to them. The time was rented. The insight: "If they can replace you, they will. If you can be replaced by a robot, you will be." Specific knowledge and equity are what can't be replaced. Everything else is a job.

**Optimizing for short-term approval.**
Naval: people optimize for appearing successful before being successful. The metric they're chasing is status, not wealth. Status is zero-sum — your win requires someone else's loss. Wealth is not zero-sum. But building wealth requires doing things that look wrong in the short term, that don't generate immediate approval, that often look like failure until they aren't. The people who optimize for appearing right usually make different choices than the people who optimize for being right.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Dispersal vs. Optionality** → Three revenue streams at a third of full commitment is renting time three times, not building leverage. The leverage only comes from going all the way on one thing.
- **Mistaking Learning for Action** → Naval: "Read what you love until you love to read." Then ship something. Consuming without producing is not building specific knowledge — it's gathering inputs for knowledge that never gets applied.
- **Performing the Journey** → "Say it in one sentence." The person who needs long explanations of what they're building is performing the building more than doing it.
- **The Gap Between Stated Values and Actual Calendar** → Health first, relationships second, work third. The calendar reveals which one is actually first.
- **Motion vs. Momentum** → The equity question cuts through all activity: "Does this move you toward ownership, or does it rent your time?"
