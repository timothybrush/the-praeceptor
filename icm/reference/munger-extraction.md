## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Identify the incentive structure before accepting the analysis.**
Munger's first diagnostic: "What are the incentives of the person giving me this advice?" The gallbladder surgeon who loved his patients also removed thousands of normal gallbladders — not from malice, but because the incentive structure made removal feel like the caring thing to do. Every management consultant report ends with the same conclusion: more management consulting is needed. They're not lying. The incentive structure makes that conclusion feel true. The move: before accepting any diagnosis, ask whose interests are served by that diagnosis.

**2. Invert first — ask what would guarantee failure.**
Munger's core method: don't start with "how do I succeed?" Start with "what would guarantee failure?" Invert the problem completely. List every reliable path to ruin. Then don't do those things. The insight: it's much easier to identify certain failure paths than to identify certain success paths. The negative is more tractable. The move: "What would guarantee that this ends badly? What would you have to do to make certain this fails?"

**3. Check for consistency and commitment bias.**
The human mind is like an egg — once a conclusion gets in, the mind hardens so no other conclusion can enter. Publicly stating a conclusion pounds it in further. When someone has announced a thesis publicly, every subsequent piece of evidence gets processed by a mind whose primary job is to defend that thesis, not evaluate it. The iron test: "Can you state the strongest argument against your current position? Not a weak version — the real one, better than the people who hold it?" If they can't, their mind has hardened.

**4. Watch for the lollapalooza — four tendencies combining.**
Munger's most important warning: when multiple psychological biases operate simultaneously toward the same end, the result is catastrophic. A Tupperware party combines reciprocation, commitment, social proof, and scarcity simultaneously — that's why it's so effective. The same combination that makes Tupperware parties work makes financial bubbles, cult behavior, and disastrous board decisions. The move: when something seems overwhelmingly obvious and everyone agrees — ask how many psychological biases are pointing the same direction.

**5. Never hire your thinking done without independently checking the why.**
Munger's prescription: when you must rely on an advisor, either apply a "windage factor" for their bias — or learn enough about their field to make them explain their reasoning. Forstein knew Good-friend was wrong but couldn't convince him because he didn't tell him WHY vividly enough. He should have said: "You will ruin your life, disgrace your family, and lose your money — is this worth it?" Vivid evidence works. Abstract moral argument doesn't. The move: always lead with consequences, not principles.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "What are the incentives of the person giving you this analysis?" — Incentive bias check.
- "What would guarantee this fails? List every reliable path to ruin." — Inversion.
- "State the strongest argument against your current position — not a weak version. Better than the people who hold it." — Consistency bias check.
- "How many psychological biases are currently pointing in the same direction?" — Lollapalooza alert.
- "Have you told them WHY vividly enough — consequences, not principles?" — Forstein's mistake.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**Federal Express and the night shift.**
FedEx required all packages to pass through one hub each night. The system had no integrity if the shift ran slow. They tried moral persuasion. Nothing worked. Someone noticed they were paying the night crew by the hour — which meant slow was rewarded. Switch to pay-per-shift: the problem disappeared immediately. The move: before trying harder at the same approach, check the incentive structure. If incentives reward the wrong behavior, no amount of moral persuasion changes the outcome.

**The Gutfreund / Salomon Compliance Failure.**
Good-friend knew his trader had lied to the government. He didn't report it. The trader had made the firm a billion dollars. He had a wife and children. Good-friend was sitting across from him. Reciprocation tendency. Liking bias. The trader's clique effect. Four or five psychological tendencies all pointing toward: don't act. Good-friend didn't act. The trader did it again. Salomon nearly collapsed. The insight: when every instinct says "let it go," check how many psychological biases are producing that feeling.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → Incentive-caused bias: the CFOs who took Intel's capital and returned it to shareholders rather than building factories were not wrong on the numbers. The incentive structure made that the smart move. The bias was structural, not personal.
- **Performing the Journey** → Social proof: everyone is building the personal brand. That's data about what the crowd is doing — not about what works. When everyone is doing the same thing, ask independently: does this actually work?
- **Mistaking Learning for Action** → Consistency/commitment: once you've announced you're "doing the research," the mind hardens. The research phase becomes self-perpetuating. Ask: what result is this learning producing?
