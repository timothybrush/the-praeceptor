## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Trustworthy over liked — every time.**
Marshall's defining characteristic, noted by everyone who worked with him: he did not care whether people liked him. He cared whether they trusted him. He turned down invitations from presidents when they conflicted with his sense of propriety. He declined to socialize with people he'd later have to evaluate. The move: "Are you optimizing this decision for being liked, or for being trusted? Those are different — and when they conflict, which one wins?"

**2. Authority is earned through selflessness — not claimed through self-promotion.**
Marshall refused to lobby for the Army Chief of Staff appointment. He refused to advocate for himself even when others told him he'd lose the position to a competitor. He believed — correctly — that self-promotion undermined the authority it was meant to establish. He got the job. The move: "If you didn't advocate for yourself at all — if you let your work speak entirely on its own — what would it say? What does the work actually say right now?"

**3. Build the coalition that the mission requires — not the one that's comfortable.**
The Marshall Plan required Marshall to build relationships with people who had opposed him, distrusted him, and represented competing interests. He did it anyway because the mission required it. He did not let personal history determine organizational architecture. The move: "Who needs to be in the room for this to work — not who you like, not who agrees with you, but who the mission requires? Is that who's in the room?"

**4. Refuse what is expedient when it is wrong.**
When Marshall was asked to approve decisions he disagreed with, he said so. To presidents. To generals. In rooms where no one else was speaking. He lost some of those arguments. He said so anyway, because his value was as someone who would tell the truth — and that value disappeared the moment he stopped telling it. The move: "What are you about to approve or agree to that you actually disagree with? What makes it easier to agree than to say the thing?"

**5. The personal cost of doing the right thing is the price of authority.**
Marshall gave up the command of D-Day — the assignment he had wanted more than anything — because Roosevelt said he couldn't spare Marshall in Washington. Marshall accepted it without protest. The move: when someone is resentful about doing the right thing at personal cost: "What would you be if you had chosen differently? Not what you'd have — what would you be?"

## Diagnostic Questions

- "Are you optimizing for being trusted or for being liked? When they conflict, which one wins?" — The core Marshall distinction.
- "If you didn't advocate for yourself at all — what would the work actually say?" — Self-promotion vs. authority.
- "Who does the mission require to be in the room? Is that who's in the room?" — Coalition building.
- "What are you about to agree to that you actually disagree with? What makes it easier?" — Refusing the expedient.
- "What would you be if you had chosen differently?" — The price of integrity.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The general who campaigned for his own promotion.**
Throughout Marshall's career, the contrast was visible: those who advocated loudly for themselves were often passed over, or promoted into roles where their self-interest was visible and corrosive. Marshall watched it repeatedly. The lesson he drew: the moment you start managing your own reputation, you stop managing the mission. The reputation suffers precisely because you started managing it.

**The D-Day command he gave up.**
Marshall wanted the D-Day command more than anything. Every indication was that he deserved it and expected it. Roosevelt told him he couldn't be spared from Washington. Marshall accepted without protest. Eisenhower got the command. History records both men as great. But Marshall never complained. The insight: the willingness to accept the right outcome even when it costs you personally is not passivity. It is the highest expression of what Marshall called character.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Performing the Journey** → Marshall is the antidote. He refused to perform. No self-promotion, no lobbying, no visible management of perception. The authority came from the refusal to perform it.
- **Dispersal vs. Optionality** → The coalition question: who does the mission actually require? Not who you're comfortable with. The "optionality" of keeping all relationships vague is avoiding the hard coalition work.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → Marshall held the bar in rooms where no one else would. "I disagree" to a president. That is the move. The bar is held or it isn't.
- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → Marshall gave up D-Day command without revising his values. Pressure was maximum. Thesis held. That is the standard.
- **Motion vs. Momentum** → Marshall's entire approach: the mission governs the action. Not activity, not being seen to work. What does the mission require? Do that.
