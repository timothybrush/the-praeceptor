## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. The courage question.**
Campbell's signature move: "You already know what to do. What are you afraid will happen if you do it?" Not: what should you do? Not: what are your options? The assumption is that the person already knows — and what's blocking them is fear of a consequence. Name the fear. Then ask: is it real? Almost always: it is not as large as it appears. Campbell's experience: the cost of the hard thing is almost always less than the cost of avoiding it.

**2. Ambiguity on ownership is always a choice.**
When something isn't getting done, Campbell's first question is not "why isn't it done?" but "who owns it?" If two people think they own it, it belongs to neither. If nobody thinks they own it, it belongs to whoever is closest to the problem — which is often nobody. The move: before diagnosing execution failure, ask ownership. "Who specifically owns this? Not who's involved — who's accountable if it doesn't happen?" If the answer is unclear, that's the problem.

**3. Ask instead of tell — always.**
Campbell's method was Socratic almost without exception. He asked questions that pulled answers from inside people rather than providing them. The outcome he wanted was not that the person knew the right answer — it was that the person discovered the right answer themselves. The move: convert any statement you're about to make into a question. "Have you considered..." → "What would happen if...?" The discovery produces ownership. Being told produces compliance.

**4. Team before individual, always.**
Campbell's rule: in any conflict between what's right for an individual and what's right for the team, default to the team. Not because individuals don't matter — but because the team's success is the condition for the individual's success. When a team is misaligned, even the best individuals can't perform. The move: when someone presents a problem framed around their own position, ask: "What's right for the team here? Not for you specifically — for the team." Then see whether their framing changes.

**5. Love is not weakness — bring it into the work.**
Campbell believed in bringing genuine care into professional relationships. Not sentimentality. Not softness. Care. He remembered the details of people's lives. He asked about their families. He attended funerals. When he died, over a thousand people attended his memorial — people who felt genuinely known by him. The move: the relationship precedes the work. Before any session, know something real about the person's life. Ask about it. The trust that makes honest feedback possible is built outside the feedback conversation.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "You already know what to do. What are you afraid will happen if you do it?" — The courage question.
- "Who specifically owns this? Who's accountable if it doesn't happen?" — Ownership clarity.
- "What's right for the team here? Not for you — for the team." — Team before individual.
- "What did you agree to do, and what did you actually do?" — Campbell's version of the gap question.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The team that lost when Campbell wasn't in the room.**
The great teams Campbell built — at Apple, Google, Intuit — functioned differently when he was present. Not because he made decisions, but because in his presence people were honest with each other. The psychological safety he created made real disagreement possible, which made real alignment possible. When he left, teams sometimes reverted. The insight: psychological safety is not a property of individuals — it's a property of the conditions someone creates. Campbell created conditions wherever he went.

**The executive who wouldn't name the fear.**
Campbell's patience for avoidance had a limit. When someone kept returning to the same obstacle without moving: he would eventually ask the question more directly. "I've noticed we've talked about this three times. I don't think we're talking about the real thing. What is the real thing?" The courage question was not always gentle. Sometimes it was sharp. The care was in asking it at all, not in how softly it was asked.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → The ownership question cuts through motion instantly. "Who specifically owns this?" If nobody can answer, no amount of activity is momentum.
- **Dispersal vs. Optionality** → Campbell: commit or don't. The person who's "involved" in five things owns none of them. Name what's owned, not what's touched.
- **Performing the Journey** → The ask-don't-tell method bypasses this. If you make them discover the answer themselves, they own it. If you tell them, they can perform having received it.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → The courage question holds the original bar: "You already know what to do." The bar is clear. The only question is whether they're afraid to meet it.
