# Ed Catmull — Mentor Mechanics Extraction
## Source: Creativity, Inc. — Overcoming the Unseen Forces That Stand in the Way of True Inspiration

---

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Surface the hidden problem first.**
Catmull's core operating principle at Pixar: "We acknowledge we will always have problems, many of them hidden from our view; and we work hard to uncover these problems, even if doing so means making ourselves uncomfortable." The move: before asking what the person is trying to accomplish, ask what they're *not* seeing. The most dangerous forces in any system are the ones that are invisible to it. "What problem exists in your situation that you currently have no language for?"

**2. Separate the work from the people doing it.**
Bad work does not mean bad people. At Pixar, every film starts as something ugly — what Catmull calls "the ugly baby." The instinct to protect the ugly baby (the early idea, the rough draft, the nascent direction) from feedback is the instinct to protect the person, not the work. The move: name this distinction explicitly. "I'm not evaluating you. I'm evaluating this piece of work at this moment in time. Those are different things." Then give the feedback that won't survive ruinous empathy.

**3. Build the feedback structure without authority.**
Pixar's Braintrust: a group of trusted peers gives notes on a film — but the director is not required to follow them. No authority. No chain of command. The feedback is good because it's disconnected from power. The move: create the conditions for honest feedback by removing consequences from the giving of it. "What would you say if you knew I couldn't fire you for saying it?"

**4. Make the invisible visible — name the blocking force.**
Catmull's core belief: "I believe the best managers acknowledge and make room for what they do not know." When someone is stuck, their stated explanation for being stuck is almost never the real one. The real one is usually something they haven't admitted to themselves yet — fear of failure, fear of success, a constraint they've internalized as permanent that isn't. The move: "What is the force in your situation that you haven't named yet? Not the problem. The thing behind the problem."

**5. Stop and audit — the Notes Day move.**
When a system is producing the wrong results and the people inside it can't see why: stop all production. Give everyone permission to examine the system that contains them. Catmull called it Notes Day — the entire studio stopped production for one day to audit everything. The move: "When did you last stop everything and ask whether the system around you is working?" If the answer is never, the system is running them, not the other way around.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "What problem exists in your situation that you currently have no language for?" — The hidden forces move.
- "What would you say if you knew there were no consequences for saying it?" — The Braintrust condition.
- "When did you last stop everything and audit whether the system is working?" — Notes Day diagnostic.
- "Are you protecting the work or protecting yourself?" — Separating the ugly baby from the person.
- "What force in your situation haven't you named yet?" — The invisible blocker.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The post-Toy Story emptiness.**
After Toy Story's success — the culmination of a 20-year goal — Catmull realized he felt adrift. He'd built a train and laid its track. The thought of "merely driving it" no longer engaged him. This is the danger of goal-completion as identity: the achieved goal leaves a void, and the void doesn't announce itself as a problem. It shows up as lack of motivation, loss of direction, a vague sense that something is wrong. The move: "What happens to your sense of purpose after this thing you're working toward is done?"

**The Pixar success that nearly destroyed its culture.**
The cultural force that threatens every successful creative team: success breeds risk aversion. What worked once becomes the template. The ugly baby gets protected earlier. Feedback gets softer. The very things that created the success start to disappear. Catmull watched this happen at every tech company he observed and built Pixar's structures specifically to fight it. The move: "What aspects of what made you successful are you now protecting in ways that will eventually undermine you?"

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → The ugly baby problem: protecting a direction that isn't working because admitting it isn't working feels like failure. Name the problem first; then assess the direction.
- **Performing the Journey** → Catmull's question for every team: are you creating or narrating creation? The Braintrust was built to distinguish between the two.
- **Mistaking Learning for Action** → Notes Day is the opposite: stop learning about the system and audit it directly. The learning must produce a change in the system or it was just learning.
- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → Risk aversion after success is how the thesis gets rewritten — each iteration slightly safer, slightly smaller. Catmull built structures specifically to resist this.
- **The Gap Between Stated Values and Actual Calendar** → "What's the one day you stop everything and examine whether your system is working?" If the calendar has never made room for that, the stated value of self-correction is not real.
