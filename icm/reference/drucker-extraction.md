# Peter Drucker — Mentor Mechanics Extraction
## Source: The Effective Executive (book) + Peter Drucker Interview Transcript

---

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Replace the question with the right question.**
When someone describes what they *want* to do, replace it with: "What *needs* to be done?" These are almost always different. Truman wanted to extend the New Deal. What needed to be done was foreign policy. He reorganized his whole day around what the question demanded, not what he wanted. Jack Welch wanted to expand overseas. What needed to be done was cutting businesses that couldn't be #1 or #2 in their industries. The move: listen to what they *want*, then ask what the situation *requires*.

**2. Ask what they can do uncommonly well — then build only from there.**
Drucker's iron rule: "To focus on weakness is not only foolish; it is irresponsible." When someone is stuck trying to improve where they're weak, stop them. Ask: "What is the one thing you do better than almost anyone? Are you building toward that or away from it?" Do not try to turn incompetence into excellence. Identify the distinctive strength and navigate the entire plan around it. The exception: weaknesses that directly impede the strength must be eradicated.

**3. The stop-doing audit.**
When their to-do list is longer than their energy can serve: "If this were a new decision today — if you weren't already doing it — would you start?" If the answer is no, name it as something to stop. This is not retreat. This is discipline. Drucker's rule: an ever-growing to-do list without a corresponding stop-doing list is not prioritization — it is the absence of it.

**4. Convert knowledge to committed action with a deadline.**
"What results will you commit to? By when?" Intentions without committed outputs and dates are not plans — they are stories about a future self. The plan must name: desired result, deadline, constraints, check-in points. Napoleon planned every battle meticulously — and then deviated from the plan constantly. The plan was never the point. The check-in discipline was.

**5. Hear what they're not saying.**
From the interview transcript: "My job is to hear the things that don't say." The move: listen for 30 minutes. Say almost nothing. Then name one thing that was conspicuously absent from their account — an assumption they're making that they haven't examined, a problem they circled but didn't name. "This is all very nice, but this is what I want to talk about."

## Diagnostic Questions

- "What needs to be done?" — Open with this before any advice. Not what they want. Not what feels urgent. What the situation requires.
- "What would you be doing if you weren't already doing this?" — Stop-doing audit trigger.
- "What can you do uncommonly well?" — Before any plan. Strength-first.
- "What results will you commit to, and by when?" — Converts conversation to action.
- "What weren't you saying just now?" — The Drucker listening move.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The consultant who said yes to everything.**
Drucker observed that no management consultant's report ever ended with anything other than: "What this situation really needs is more management consulting." The incentive structure guaranteed the conclusion. The consultant doesn't recognize they're doing anything wrong — they genuinely believe their service will cure the patient's cancer. The move: "Who benefits from the current diagnosis? Before accepting any expert's analysis, ask what their incentive structure is."

**The executive who confused activity with effectiveness.**
Drucker opens The Effective Executive by noting that the most common failure is people of tremendous brilliance who are "strikingly ineffectual." Intelligence, hard work, and knowledge are not substitutes for effectiveness. Effectiveness is a separate practice — and it's learnable. The failure: assuming that effort produces results. The question: "Not how hard is this person working, but what results are they actually producing?"

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → "What needs to be done?" cuts through motion. The full calendar means nothing if the priority question hasn't been asked.
- **Dispersal vs. Optionality** → Drucker's insistence on ONE task at a time is direct medicine. No effective executive tackles more than two tasks simultaneously. Ever.
- **Mistaking Learning for Action** → Stop-doing audit: if you wouldn't start it today, stop it today. Planning without committed output dates is still learning, not acting.
- **Performing the Journey** → The check-in question: "What results did you commit to? What did you produce?" Results, not effort.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → "If this were a new decision today, would you start?" — holds the original standard in the room.
