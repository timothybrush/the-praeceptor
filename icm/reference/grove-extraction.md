# Andy Grove — Mentor Mechanics Extraction
## Source: High Output Management (book) + Stanford Interview Transcript (Moore's Law)

---

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Ask for the output, not the activity.**
Grove's foundational diagnostic: "What is the output of your role?" Not your responsibilities. Not your tasks. The measurable result that wouldn't exist if you weren't there. A manager's output equals their own production PLUS the output they enable in others. Most people can name their activities. Very few can name their output. When they can't, the role isn't real yet — or they've confused motion for work.

**2. Only the paranoid survive — identify the 10x force.**
Grove's rule for any established business: at any moment, a force is operating in the environment that could make your current approach irrelevant. The force is usually 10x — not a marginal improvement, but an order-of-magnitude shift. The question: "What's the 10x force that could make what you're doing irrelevant? Where is it coming from? Have you seen it? Are you paying attention to it?" People who ask this question annually rarely get surprised by inflection points. People who don't ask it always do.

**3. The strategic inflection point test.**
When a business is at a crossroads: "If we got fired today and the board brought in a new CEO — someone without our history, without our ego invested in the current direction — what would that person do?" This question removes the sunk-cost bias. It forces the right question: what does the situation require, not what are we committed to? Grove used this question to lead Intel out of the DRAM business. The history of the product they'd built made the right answer emotionally impossible. Pretending a new CEO had just arrived made it emotionally available.

**4. The one-on-one is the most important meeting a manager has.**
Grove's prescription: prepare for one-on-ones as seriously as you'd prepare for a board meeting. The purpose is information transfer — the subordinate's knowledge to the manager, not the other way. The manager's job in a one-on-one is mostly to listen and ask: "What's blocking you? What would you do differently? What do you think I should know that I don't know?" The meeting belongs to the subordinate.

**5. Apply leverage to high-leverage activities.**
Grove's time economics: some activities have enormous leverage (preparing a training program that teaches hundreds) and some have almost none (attending a meeting where your presence doesn't change the outcome). The discipline: before any commitment of time, ask: "What's the leverage of this activity? How many people does this decision or this action affect?" Time spent on high-leverage activities compounds. Time spent on low-leverage activities disappears.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "What is the output of your role? The measurable result that wouldn't exist if you weren't here?" — Grove's output question.
- "What's the 10x force that could make what you're building irrelevant? Have you looked?" — Paranoia check.
- "If a new CEO arrived tomorrow with no history here, what would they do?" — Strategic inflection point.
- "What's the leverage of this activity — how many people does it affect?" — Time economics.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**Intel and the DRAM business.**
Intel built the DRAM chip. It was their identity. By the mid-1980s, Japanese manufacturers were making better chips more cheaply. Intel was losing the business. But DRAM was what they were — walking away felt like walking away from themselves. Grove asked: if a new CEO arrived, what would he do? The answer was obvious. Leave DRAM. Focus on microprocessors. They did it. It became the basis for everything that followed. The failure would have been staying — not from lack of information, but from inability to act on what the information said.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → The output question cuts through motion directly. A full schedule of meetings is not output. The measurable result is.
- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → The 10x force often appears while the financials still look fine. The paranoia check is run BEFORE the numbers turn bad, not after.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → The one-on-one with the output question holds the standard: "What result did you commit to? What result did you produce?"
