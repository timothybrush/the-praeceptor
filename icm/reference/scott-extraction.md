## Source: Radical Candor (book) + TED Talk Transcript

## The 5 Behavioral Moves

**1. Name the quadrant — which failure mode is operating?**
Scott's 2x2: care personally × challenge directly. Every failure to communicate honestly falls into one of three failure quadrants: Obnoxious Aggression (challenge without care — people go into fight-or-flight and literally can't hear you), Manipulative Insincerity (neither care nor challenge — back-stabbing, passive aggression, the most toxic workplace behavior), or Ruinous Empathy (care without challenge — the most common failure, disguised as kindness). Before any feedback conversation, name which failure mode you're at risk of entering. Then choose Radical Candor instead.

**2. Say the one thing directly and then stop talking.**
The move: "Name it directly and specifically. Then stop talking." Not: "I have some concerns about your work, and I want to first acknowledge that you're doing a lot of great things, and I really appreciate you, but there are a few areas where..." That is Ruinous Empathy. The actual move: say the specific thing. Don't soften with false positives. Don't qualify. Don't restate. Say the thing and wait. The silence that follows is not a problem to fill. It's where the other person processes what they just heard.

**3. When furious, get curious.**
When the other person responds to feedback with anger: your instinct is to defend yourself, escalate, or retreat. None of these are correct. The anger is a signal — there's something you haven't understood yet. "Get curious, not furious." The move: move up on the care dimension, not the challenge dimension. "Why is this person so angry?" is the question. The answer changes the response. Most anger during feedback is about something other than the feedback itself.

**4. Solicit before dishing.**
Radical candor always starts with receiving, not giving. "Don't dish it out before you prove you can take it." Before giving anyone feedback, ask them for feedback on you. What am I doing that makes your work harder? What should I stop doing? The inverse dynamic: when a leader is known to receive feedback well, honest upward feedback becomes possible. When they're not, everyone manages upward — which means the leader never gets accurate information.

**5. Measure at the listener's ear, not the speaker's mouth.**
Radical candor is not a property of the feedback you give — it's a property of how it lands. "Radical candor gets measured not in the speaker's mouth but at the listener's ear." If someone seems sad after feedback: move up on care. If someone seems angry: get curious, move up on care. If someone brushes off feedback: push harder on challenge. The response tells you what adjustment is needed, if you're paying attention.

## Diagnostic Questions

- "Which quadrant are you operating in right now — which failure mode?" — Before any feedback conversation.
- "What haven't you said to this person that you know they need to hear?" — Ruinous empathy reveal.
- "When they push back, what's your instinct? Run it through the failure quadrant." — Aggression vs. curiosity.
- "When did you last ask this person for feedback on you?" — Solicitation before dishing.

## Failure Stories from This Source

**The Alex story — ten months.**
Alex was smart, charming, funny — and his work was terrible. Kim Scott said nothing. For ten months. She liked him. She was afraid he'd cry, and afraid people would think she was cruel. She convinced herself she was being kind. Eventually she had to let him go. His response: "Why didn't you tell me? Why didn't anyone tell me? I thought you all cared about me." Two parts to her failure: Ruinous Empathy (she cared but wouldn't challenge) and a trace of Manipulative Insincerity (she was also worried about her own reputation as a leader). The kindness that felt like care stole ten months of his professional life. He agreed he should go — but by then his reputation on the team was shot.

**The team that drifted to ruinous empathy.**
Small groups often start radically candid — they know each other well, honesty comes easily. Then success creates growth. Growth creates social distance. Social distance creates ruinous empathy. Then somebody finally bursts out and says the thing — probably not well, but it works. And because it works, they do it again. And because everyone else is determined to be nice, they excuse it: "He didn't mean harm." The next thing you know, "the jerks begin to win, and that is when the culture begins to lose." The slide from Radical Candor to Ruinous Empathy to Obnoxious Aggression to Manipulative Insincerity is how organizations lose the ability to tell themselves the truth.

## Seven Blind Spot Connections

- **Motion vs. Momentum** → Ruinous empathy produces a culture of comfortable meetings with no honest information exchange. Everything looks fine. Nothing is improving.
- **Performing the Journey** → The leader who is kind to everyone and challenges no one is performing leadership. Real leadership is the uncomfortable conversation you had today.
- **Financial Pressure Rewriting the Thesis** → When the team is in crisis and the leader still can't give direct feedback — the bar has been lowered quietly. They're now managing for comfort, not for results.
- **Lowering the Bar Quietly** → Every time you decide not to say the hard thing, you are lowering the bar by one increment. The accumulation is the culture. Name it before it normalizes.
